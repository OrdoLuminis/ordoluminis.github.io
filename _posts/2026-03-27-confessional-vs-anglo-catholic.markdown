<p style="text-indent:2em;">
Anglicanism is not a monolith. Within its broad historical tradition exist streams so theologically distinct that they represent fundamentally different answers to the most basic questions of Christian doctrine — authority, salvation, the sacraments, and the meaning of the Reformation itself. <strong>The Order of Light stands within the Confessional Anglican tradition</strong>, a position that differs from Anglo-Catholicism not merely in emphasis or tone but across every major theological category. Understanding that difference requires clarity on what Anglo-Catholicism actually is and what Confessional Anglicanism actually claims.</p>

## On Authority
**Anglo-Catholic:** Scripture interpreted through apostolic tradition and the magisterium of the church. Tradition carries independent doctrinal authority alongside Scripture.

**Confessional Anglican:** Scripture is the primary authority, with confessions serving as faithful summaries of scriptural teaching. The Reformation's doctrinal reforms are explicitly embraced, not bracketed.

## On Justification
**Anglo-Catholic:** Justification is typically understood as a process involving infused righteousness — grace communicated through the sacraments gradually transforms the believer. Rome's Council of Trent defined this position explicitly against Protestant forensic justification.

**Confessional Anglican:** Justification is forensic — Christ's righteousness imputed to the believer as a legal declaration. The transformationist emphasis belongs to sanctification flowing from union with Christ, not to justification itself being a transformative process.

## On the Sacraments
**Anglo-Catholic:** Sacraments are instruments that actually convey grace — baptismal regeneration, eucharistic presence, sacerdotal priesthood. The priest mediates grace through sacramental acts.

**Confessional Anglican:** Sacraments are signs and seals rather than regenerating instruments. The Order of Light explicitly rejects sacramental regeneration through the Declaration of Principles, affirming that regeneration is the sovereign work of the Spirit rather than an effect of the sacramental act itself.

## On Union with Christ
**Anglo-Catholic:** Participatory language is used but tends toward ontological absorption — theosis in a quasi-Catholic direction, typically mediated through the Eucharist as the primary locus of union.

**Confessional Anglican:** Union with Christ is soteriological and Spirit-worked — forensic justification and real transformation both flow from genuine spiritual union with Christ himself, not from sacramental mediation. Union is the trunk from which all benefits of salvation grow as branches.

## On the Reformation
**Anglo-Catholic:** The Reformation is at best a tragic rupture to be overcome through ecumenical retrieval, at worst a heretical deviation from apostolic continuity.

**Confessional Anglican:** The Reformation's doctrinal reforms are explicitly and positively embraced as recoveries of biblical truth obscured by medieval accretion. The Order of Light stands in direct continuity with the Reformed Anglican tradition that produced the Thirty-Nine Articles, the Declaration of Principles, and the work of figures like Cranmer, Jewel, and Davenant.

## On Atonement
**Anglo-Catholic:** Atonement theology tends toward participatory and sacrificial categories — the Mass understood as a re-presentation of the sacrifice of Christ. Penal substitutionary atonement is frequently downplayed or rejected as an overly juridical framework.

**Confessional Anglican:** The Order of Light follows Davenant's hypothetical universalism, which presupposes genuine penal substitutionary atonement — Christ actually bearing the penalty for sin on behalf of all humanity provisionally and the elect efficaciously. The atonement happened once, historically, on the cross — not re-presented in any liturgical act.

## The Simplest Summary

Anglo-Catholicism is essentially Roman Catholicism that retained English liturgy and rejected papal supremacy while preserving sacramental theology, infused grace, and tradition as co-equal authority alongside Scripture. The Reformation for Anglo-Catholics is at best an embarrassment to be managed.

The Order of Light is genuinely and unapologetically Protestant — forensic justification, Scripture's primacy, and the Reformation's doctrinal reforms positively embraced — expressed through Anglican liturgical forms rather than Presbyterian or Baptist ones. It inhabits Anglican worship while standing firmly in the Reformed evangelical tradition that produced that worship in the first place.

The distinction matters because Anglicanism's breadth can obscure real and consequential theological differences. Not all Anglicans mean the same thing when they speak of grace, sacrament, or salvation. The Order of Light means something precise — and that precision is the point.
