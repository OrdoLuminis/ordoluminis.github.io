# The Father's Counsel: Success in the Face of Envy

<p style="text-indent:2em;">Jesus said, <em>"Blessed is the one who is not offended by me"</em> (ESV Study Bible, 2008, Matt. 11:6; cf. Lk. 7:23), and, <em>"The world cannot hate you, but it hates me because I testify about it that its works are evil"</em> (ESV Study Bible, 2008, Jn. 7:7). The Christian is called not only to bear fruit but to understand why the bearing of fruit provokes hostility — and to respond with the wisdom that comes from above rather than from below.</p>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">This is not primarily a rebuke. It is an invitation to clarity — about what success actually is, why it provokes envy, and what the Spirit produces in those who are genuinely united to Christ.</p>

---

<p style="text-indent:2em;">Envy is not simply a character flaw. It is a spiritual symptom — evidence that the sufficiency of union with Christ has not yet penetrated a person's measure of worth. As intelligence grows, so too does humility, and those who speak ill of others are merely projecting their own insecurities and limitations onto those they envy. As Seneca (1889) vividly illustrates in his <em>Minor Dialogues, On the Tranquility of the Mind (De Tranquillitate Animi)</em>:</p>

<blockquote>...hence comes the state of mind of those who loathe their idleness, complain that they have nothing to do, and view the progress of others with the bitterest jealousy: for an unhappy sloth favours the growth of envy, and those who cannot succeed themselves wish everyone else to be ruined (Seneca, 1889, p. 257).</blockquote>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">What Seneca diagnosed as philosophical disorder, Scripture diagnoses as spiritual poverty. The person who measures worth by material accumulation has no stable ground — and when another prospers, their own standing feels threatened. This is why Dittmar et al. (2014) found that strong materialistic values are reliably associated with lower life satisfaction, greater social comparison, and poorer mental well-being. The research confirms what Proverbs already declared: <em>"Wealth is worthless in the day of wrath"</em> (Pr. 11:4 ESV).</p>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">The Christian understanding goes further. In the same way that the upper class Pharisees envied the materially poor Christ, materialists also persecute the spiritually rich among us (Jas. 1:9-11; Matt. 27:18; 25:40). The Pharisees envied Christ not because he was rich, but because he was genuinely righteous — and genuine righteousness is a rebuke to those who have only its appearance (ESV Study Bible, 2008, Matt. 27:18). In the same way, Spirit-worked transformation in a believer's life — real change, real love, real integrity — is quietly confrontational to those who have substituted material success for spiritual substance. The envy this provokes is not really about money or status. It is about the unbearable visibility of someone who has found their sufficiency elsewhere.</p>

---

<p style="text-indent:2em;">For both the Christian and the Stoic, true success has always spoken for itself in the form of <em>aretē</em> — moral excellence (ESV Study Bible, 2008, 2 Pet. 1:5). But for the Christian, aretē is not a self-cultivated achievement. It is the visible fruit of genuine union with Christ — the Spirit necessarily and freely producing what the self cannot manufacture:</p>

<blockquote>I am the vine; you are the branches. Whoever abides in me and I in him, he it is that bears much fruit, for apart from me you can do nothing (ESV Study Bible, 2008, Jn. 15:5).</blockquote>

<blockquote>But the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control (New Living Translation, 2015, Gal. 5:22–23).</blockquote>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">This is the transformationist heart of genuine Christian success: not the accumulation of external goods, but the progressive renewal of the whole person after the image of Christ — love that does not envy, contentment that does not compare, generosity that does not hoard (ESV Study Bible, 2008, 1 Cor. 13:4; Phil. 4:11–12). The person being genuinely transformed by union with Christ does not need to diminish others to establish their own worth, because their worth is no longer at stake in the same way. They have found it elsewhere, and in someone else.</p>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">This is what Paul meant: <em>"I have learned, in whatever situation I am, to be content"</em> (ESV Study Bible, 2008, Phil. 4:11). Contentment is not passivity. It is the fruit of a settled identity — one no longer subject to the social comparisons that feed envy.</p>

---

<p style="text-indent:2em;">A good father teaches his son to anchor his identity here first. He teaches him to care about his relationship with Christ enough to abide by his teachings — not to say <em>"I don't worry about it,"</em> which is the self-deception of the unreflective (ESV Study Bible, 2008, 1 Jn. 3:7). But he also teaches him, practically, to build on solid ground:</p>

<blockquote>Establish your work outside and get your fields ready; afterward build your house (NET Bible, 2019, Pr. 24:27).</blockquote>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">The NET Bible's note is worth observing: if "house" is figurative for household, the proverb counsels financial preparation before family formation. Wisdom that takes the creation order seriously does not despise practical provision — it orders it rightly. Education, diligence, and moral excellence together produce what no amount of envy can counterfeit: a life that actually works, for the sake of those entrusted to one's care.</p>

---

<p style="text-indent:2em;">Finally, heed the simplest counsel Jesus ever gave on the subject of comparing one's path to another's:</p>

<blockquote>Peter asked Jesus, "What about him, Lord?" Jesus replied, "If I want him to remain alive until I return, what is that to you? As for you, follow me" (New Living Translation, 2015, John 21:21–22).</blockquote>

<p style="text-indent:2em;">What the Spirit produces in another person's life is not your measure. What he is producing in yours is. The freedom that comes from genuinely grasping this — from having one's identity so rooted in union with Christ that another's flourishing becomes a cause for gratitude rather than threat — is itself one of the clearest marks of genuine transformation.</p>
